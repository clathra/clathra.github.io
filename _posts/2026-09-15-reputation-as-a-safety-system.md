A shipping company holds defences against the worst outcomes of its trade. Some are technical, residing in hull design, machinery, and navigation systems. Others are procedural, written into checklists, drills, and the chain of command. A further class of defence is less often counted in the same ledger, although it operates on the same logic of preparedness. A firm's reputation, its relationships, and its prepared communications form part of how it absorbs the shock of an incident. The work of Georgakis and Alexopoulos (2017), presented in the proceedings of the International Conference on Strategic Innovative Marketing, offers a frame for treating this material as part of a safety system.

## Proactive and reactive marketing

Georgakis and Alexopoulos (2017) examine the role of marketing in shipping when accidents occur, and draw a line between two kinds of action. Proactive action is taken before an incident. It builds the relationships, the standing, and the readiness that a firm can draw on later. Reactive action is taken after an incident, in the compressed hours when an event is already public and the firm is already being judged. The distinction matters because the two operate under different conditions. Proactive work is done with time, deliberation, and a clear head. Reactive work is done under pressure, with partial information, and in full view of an audience that has already formed expectations.

## How scrutiny has changed

Georgakis and Alexopoulos (2017) use a counterfactual to show how the conditions for reactive action have shifted. They point to an incident that drew limited public reaction decades ago, the 1979 collision involving the tanker Gino. An event of that kind, occurring today, would provoke a global response. Public scrutiny has risen sharply since then, and the speed at which information travels has risen with it. A maritime incident now reaches a worldwide audience within hours, carried by channels that did not exist in 1979 and amplified by a public that expects an account. The window in which a firm can shape the understanding of an event has narrowed, and the cost of being unprepared inside that window has grown.

## Preparedness built in advance

The implication follows directly. A firm's reputation, its standing relationships with response teams and port-state authorities, and its prepared communications all function as part of its resilience to an incident. Each of these is built in advance. A relationship with a response organisation has value because it exists before the call is made. The trust of a port-state authority rests on a record of conduct accumulated over years. A communications plan has worth because it was drafted, tested, and agreed while the firm could still think clearly. This preparedness cannot be assembled in the hours after an event. The moment of crisis consumes attention and time, and the relationships that matter most are the ones already in place when it begins. The material belongs to the same category as a lifeboat drill or a maintenance schedule, in that its value is realised long after the work of preparing it is done.

## The charterer's view of safety

This connects to the safety orientation of tanker charterers, documented by Plomaritou. Charterers of tankers attend closely to the safety record and conduct of the firms they hire. A vetting decision rests on evidence of how a company is run, and that evidence accumulates over time through inspections, records, and reputation. A firm whose reputation and conduct are managed in advance is better placed on two fronts. It is better placed to retain charterer approval in ordinary trading, because the same record that signals safety also signals reliability. It is better placed to withstand the scrutiny that follows any incident, because a firm with a strong prior standing meets that scrutiny from a position of established credibility. The two effects reinforce one another. The conduct that earns approval is the conduct that holds up when an event puts it to the test.

## Reputation within the safety system

The argument that emerges treats crisis communications and reputation management as an operational asset for a shipping company. They belong to the same safety system as the firm's technical and procedural defences, and they answer to the same discipline of preparedness. A hull is inspected before it fails. A drill is run before the emergency arrives. By the same logic, a reputation is built, relationships are formed, and communications are prepared before the incident that calls on them. Georgakis and Alexopoulos (2017) place this material inside the operational frame of the firm, where it sits alongside the other defences a company maintains against the consequences of an accident. A firm that manages its reputation as part of that system carries a measure of resilience that the technical and procedural layers alone do not supply.

What the frame leaves implicit is where this preparedness comes from. A reputation is produced continuously, through the record a firm publishes, the consistency of how it communicates in ordinary times, and the standing it builds with the audiences that will judge it later. That production is the work of marketing and communications, carried out in the calm periods when nothing is wrong. A firm that uses those periods to build its standing enters any future incident with its resilience already in place, and a firm that neglects them has little to draw on when the scrutiny arrives, since credibility cannot be manufactured in the hours after an event.
