## A ship manager's quiet channel

Public attention in maritime marketing tends to follow the large container lines, whose social feeds carry vessel imagery, sustainability claims and customer messaging. A study by Šekularac-Ivošević and colleagues (2023), published in STED Journal, looked elsewhere. It examined Bernhard Schulte Shipmanagement, a ship manager whose presence on public social media was lighter than that of a major container line. The finding of interest sat inside the company, away from its public profile. Bernhard Schulte Shipmanagement ran a sustained internal channel, a Seafarer Blog, organised around modules that included Success, Wellbeing and Safety.

## What the blog actually contained

The content profile, drawn from the same study and covering 2020 to 2022, shows where the effort went. Successes accounted for 72 posts. Wellbeing reached 63 and Safety also reached 63. Our Stories contributed 45, Events 36 and Insights 27. The volume and the spread matter as much as any single category. This was a programme with editorial weight behind it, maintained across three years, with the largest share given to crew achievement and the next largest to the conditions and safety of the people aboard.

The categories describe an audience. A blog weighted toward Successes, Wellbeing and Safety speaks first to seafarers and to the shore staff who support them. It records what crews accomplish, how the company attends to their welfare, and how it treats their safety. The material reads as internal communication, addressed to the workforce, and it functions as employer-brand content at the same time. The two roles are inseparable here, because the message that retains a serving crew is the same message that attracts the next one.

## Why this reads as marketing

The maritime sector faces a structural shortage of seafarers, and a parallel difficulty in recruiting and keeping shore engineers. In that setting, employer brand and internal communications are a form of marketing. The labour market is the market that constrains the business, and the work of competing in it follows marketing logic. A company tells a consistent story about its people, builds a reputation as an employer, and converts that reputation into applications, acceptances and lower attrition.

Bernhard Schulte Shipmanagement, treated here as a single-company illustration, shows the mechanism in practice. Celebrating crew achievement, attending publicly to wellbeing, and documenting safety builds retention among current crew and recruitment pull among prospective ones. The content has a second life beyond the internal audience. The same material, shared externally, becomes a reputation asset that speaks to clients evaluating a manager and to the next generation of staff weighing where to sail or where to work ashore. A client reading about how a manager treats its crews learns something about operational seriousness. A cadet reading the same material learns where careers are valued.

## The economics of telling the story

The figures behind the blog point to a programme that is modest in cost and durable in output. Producing 72 posts on crew success across three years, alongside the wellbeing, safety, stories, events and insights material, is editorial work of a kind most maritime firms already have the raw material for. Every voyage, certification, rescue drill, promotion and shore-team contribution is a potential entry. The constraint is attention and consistency, with budget a minor factor.

The comparison that gives the work its weight is the cost of doing without it. Crew turnover carries direct expense in recruitment and training, and indirect expense in lost continuity and disrupted operations. Unfilled berths and vacant engineering roles constrain what a fleet can take on. Against those costs, a sustained content programme that strengthens an employer's standing is inexpensive. A maritime firm that tells the story of its people consistently competes for talent on better terms, and it does so while building a public reputation that serves the commercial side of the business as well.

## A model worth reading carefully

The Bernhard Schulte Shipmanagement blog is one company's practice, observed in one study, and it should be read as an illustration, with a benchmark beyond its reach. Read that way, it still carries a clear lesson for a sector that under-invests in marketing and is short of the people it needs. The content that holds a workforce together and the content that markets a firm to talent and clients are the same content. Bernhard Schulte Shipmanagement organised that content around success, wellbeing and safety, kept it running for three years, and in doing so demonstrated that internal communication and employer brand belong inside the marketing function of a maritime business.
