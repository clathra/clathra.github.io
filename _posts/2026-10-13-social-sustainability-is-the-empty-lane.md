## What container lines actually say about sustainability

Altuntas Vural, Bastug and Gulmez (2021), writing in Transportation Research Part D, examined how the world's largest container shipping firms position their brands around sustainability on social media. The study coded sustainability tweets from the top-10 container lines against the Triple Bottom Line, the framework that splits sustainability into economic, environmental and social dimensions. It also coded whether each message offered a functional benefit, the utilitarian case about cost or efficiency, or an emotional benefit that appeals to identity and feeling. The sample is the ten largest carriers on a single platform, English-language, so the results read as indicative of the segment leaders within the wider industry.

The pattern in that study is concentration. Firms clustered on either economic or environmental sustainability, and the dominant message tied the two together. The "win-win" frame, in which a green measure also saves money or fuel, carried much of the sustainability communication. Slow steaming that cuts both emissions and bunker bills is the archetype. The economic and environmental pillars were busy lanes, and most carriers occupied them in similar terms.

The social pillar of sustainability was almost vacant. Of the ten firms, only one, Pacific International Lines, positioned its brand anywhere near social sustainability, the dimension covering people, communities, labour and seafarer welfare. The benefit type was lopsided in the same direction. Seven of the ten firms relied on functional, utilitarian benefits, and only a few drew on emotional benefits. The authors treat that emotional register as the larger differentiation and engagement opportunity, and they note earlier evidence that many container firms underuse social media for marketing at all.

## What audiences reward over time

A second study tracked how this messaging moved and how the public responded to it. Wang, Wong, Li and Yuen (2021), writing in Transport Policy, ran a longitudinal analysis of sustainability tweets from eight of the top-10 liners across 2016 to 2019. This sample shares the same limits, a small set of segment leaders on one English-language platform, so it too is indicative.

Over the period the firms diversified what they said. In the early window of 2016 and 2017, about 69 percent of sustainability tweets concerned "core" business responsibilities, the parts of sustainability directly bound to running ships and moving cargo. By 2018 and 2019 the mix had shifted toward roughly 46 percent core and 41 percent peripheral, with the peripheral share covering themes such as gender, clean energy and climate. The communication broadened away from the firm's operational core.

Audience response moved the other way. By 2018 and 2019 the public increasingly endorsed the core-responsibility messages, which averaged about 27.9 retweets each, a significant gap over the peripheral content. Claims anchored in the firm's actual business carried further than claims about general causes. Credibility, in this reading, tracks proximity to what the firm does. A liner talking about the emissions and welfare conditions of its own ships earned more engagement than the same liner talking about wider social themes at one remove from its operations.

## Reading the two findings together

The two studies describe the same communication space from different angles, and they agree on its shape. One lane is crowded. The economic and environmental pillars, carried by win-win green messaging and framed in functional terms, hold most of what the segment leaders say. Carriers entering that lane arrive among competitors making near-identical claims, and a green message that resembles every other green message does little to separate one brand from the next.

Two openings sit alongside that crowd. The first is the social pillar. With a single firm in the Altuntas Vural and colleagues sample positioned near it, the territory covering seafarers, crews, port communities and labour is close to uncontested. The second is the emotional register, used by only a few firms where the majority stayed functional. The two openings reinforce each other, because the social pillar is the part of sustainability most naturally expressed in human and emotional terms. A story about the people who keep a vessel running carries feeling in a way that a fuel-efficiency statistic does not.

The Wang and colleagues finding supplies the discipline that keeps either opening from collapsing into noise. Audiences rewarded claims tied to the firm's real operations and showed less appetite for peripheral causes. Occupying the social pillar works when the substance is the firm's own seafarers, its own crewing practices and the communities its own ships touch. A general statement about social good, detached from the business, sits in the peripheral category that drew weaker engagement.

## What this means for a maritime brand

For a maritime brand the combined reading reframes sustainability communication as positioning work. The annual sustainability report handles the reporting obligation and records what a firm did. The social channel decides where the brand stands in the audience's mind, and the coding in both studies maps a competitive field with crowded and empty zones. A firm choosing what to say is choosing a position on that field.

The green and win-win lane is the crowded zone, and a brand that enters it competes on sameness. The social pillar and the emotional register are the empty zones, available because most of the segment leaders have not moved into them. The condition attached is substance. The engagement gap that Wang and colleagues recorded between core and peripheral messages says that audiences reward claims fastened to the firm's own operations. A brand that speaks to its seafarers, its crews and its port communities, in human terms and from its own conduct, occupies ground its competitors have left open while meeting the credibility test the same research identifies. That combination, an uncontested social-pillar territory paired with an emotional register and backed by the firm's actual practice, is where a maritime brand can differentiate online while its competitors blend into the shared green claim.
