## What an exploratory study of Turkish maritime firms found

Baruonu and Sanri (2019), writing in the Journal of ETA Maritime Science, examined how Turkish maritime businesses used Facebook. The starting point was adoption. Of 160 firms, only 43 maintained a Facebook page, and 16 of those were inactive. Roughly 27 active pages remained out of the original 160. Most of the sector was absent from the platform, and a meaningful share of the firms that had created a page had since let it fall silent.

The study is exploratory, the active-page subset numbers fifteen, and the data covers Turkey and Facebook alone. The figures are best read as indicative of a pattern, with no claim to measure the global sector. Within that limit, the picture is consistent. The authors also report a maritime stakeholder engagement index of 18 against 52 for the broader logistics sector, a comparison drawn from Surucu and Sakar. Maritime trailed even the parent industry it belongs to.

What the active firms posted is as telling as how few of them posted. Across the active pages, content coded as corporate image accounted for around 76 percent of material. Special-days content made up 19 percent, announcements 4 percent, and sectoral news a single percent. The channels carried a steady stream of company-promotional material and almost nothing that engaged with the industry the firms operate in.

## What actually travelled

The engagement findings cut against the dominant content choice. Engagement skewed toward corporate-image content, which the firms produced in volume, yet the format and treatment of a post mattered as much as its subject. Videos out-shared photos. Posts that carried no logo or only a small one were shared far more widely than posts stamped with heavy watermarks. Posts that tagged individual staff by name were shared by those same staff, extending reach into personal networks the company page could never address directly.

The mechanism is straightforward. A heavy watermark signals advertising, and audiences share advertising sparingly. A lightly marked image or a short video reads as content worth passing on, and the platform's distribution rewards the sharing it generates. Naming a person attaches a face and an account to the post, and that person becomes a distribution channel in their own right. The firms holding their material tightly, branding every frame and keeping the people out of view, were suppressing the exact behaviour that would have carried their work further.

This connects to a wider framing in the literature. Sekularac-Ivosevic (2021), in the Journal of Innovative Business and Management, divides maritime firms into two postures toward digital marketing. Open-minded firms treat visibility and brand awareness as something to build in public. Passive firms hold back, wary that an active presence lets competitors benchmark them. The same paper identifies an opening for small operators to use digital marketing to reach markets the large firms have not covered. The Turkish data shows what the passive posture produces in practice: low adoption, dormant pages, and the firms that do post defaulting to the heavily branded corporate material that performs least well.

## The implication for maritime marketing

Two requirements sit behind organic reach, and they operate in sequence. The first is presence. A firm with no active channel collects no engagement, and the adoption figures in Baruonu and Sanri (2019) show how much of the sector still fails this first test. The second requirement is content discipline. Presence is the entry condition; the treatment of what gets posted determines whether any of it travels. A firm can clear the first bar and still suppress its own reach by watermarking every image and keeping its people anonymous.

The findings point to a specific discipline. Video earns more sharing than static images. Light branding or none travels further than a heavy watermark. Naming staff turns colleagues into distribution. Each of these works by lowering the signal that a post is an advertisement and raising the signal that it is worth passing along. Heavy-handed self-promotion produces the opposite, and it does so most reliably on the corporate-image content that maritime firms already over-produce. The firms chasing engagement through volume of branded material are working against the platform's own distribution logic.

This is the reasoning behind Clathra's own house guideline against watermarks. The discipline is to let the work and the people carry the message and to keep the branding light enough that audiences will move the content for the firm. A confident operator does not need to stamp its name across every frame, because the quality of the work and the credibility of the named individuals behind it do the persuading. The Turkish study, indicative as its sample is, gives an empirical edge to a position that might otherwise rest on taste alone. The reach a firm wants is the reach it forfeits each time it brands the content past the point an audience will share it.

For the long tail of maritime firms that Sekularac-Ivosevic (2021) describes as having room to reach uncovered markets, the lesson compounds. Small operators rarely have the budget to buy attention at the scale of a major line. Organic reach is the affordable channel, and it responds to restraint. The firms that show their work plainly, put their people forward, and resist the urge to brand everything are positioned to earn the distribution that paid promotion would otherwise cost them.
