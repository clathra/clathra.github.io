A common assumption in maritime firms holds that better online marketing follows from better tools and larger budgets. A study of online marketing in B2B maritime logistics firms by Hosseinzadeh Bakhtouri and colleagues (2026), published in Digital Transformation and Administration Innovation, points in another direction. Across the factors the authors measured, the strongest drivers of marketing performance were digital readiness and management commitment, and management commitment scored highest of all. The journal is a lower-tier outlet and its data are self-reported, so the figures should be read as indicative support for the pattern they describe.

## What the study measured

The authors set out to identify which conditions separate maritime logistics firms that market well online from those that struggle. Their results place two factors at the front. Digital readiness, meaning the organisational capacity to adopt and sustain online channels, came through as a leading driver. Management commitment ranked above every other factor measured. The signal in the data is that performance tracks the conditions a firm creates for itself, ahead of the specific platforms or spend it applies.

The same study identifies the principal drag on performance. Technological barriers, such as fragmented legacy systems, weighed on the firms in the sample. Maritime operations accumulate these systems over decades, and the integration cost is real. The constraint the authors describe sits in readiness and leadership ahead of budget or tooling. A firm can carry old systems and still market well when the organisation is prepared to do the work and the leadership stands behind it.

## Why leadership ranks first

Marketing maturity in this sector is a management question first and a tooling question second. The reason is structural. Tools can be bought in a quarter. Readiness is built over longer periods through staffing, process, and the steady attention of people who decide where the firm spends its effort. Management commitment supplies that attention. When leadership treats the public presence of the firm as a standing priority, the budget, the staff time, and the editorial discipline tend to follow. When leadership treats it as an afterthought, the most capable platform sits idle.

This explains a pattern visible across the sector. A maritime firm does not have to be fully digital to market well. It needs leadership willing to stand behind a credible, modern presence and the organisational readiness to maintain it. A modest site kept current, a set of pages that answer the questions a charterer or a port actually asks, and a steady cadence of updates will carry a firm further than an expensive build left to age.

## How the difference shows in practice

The practical consequence is a competitive one. A firm whose leadership treats its public presence as a priority will out-market a better-resourced competitor whose leadership treats it as an afterthought. The difference shows in execution. Consistency, accuracy, and quality come from sustained organisational effort, and that effort is a leadership choice before it is a line item. The under-invested firm is rarely held back by the price of software. It is held back by the absence of a decision at the top that the work matters enough to be done well and done regularly.

For operators that have historically under-invested in marketing, the implication is clarifying. The first audit worth running is internal. It examines whether the firm has the readiness to sustain an online presence and whether its leadership is prepared to commit to one. Those two conditions, in the pattern the study describes, account for more of the result than the marketing stack a firm chooses to assemble.

## The management reading

The finding reframes a budget conversation as a management one. Spend allocated without readiness and commitment produces tools that go unused and campaigns that lapse. Readiness and commitment, applied even to modest resources, produce a presence that compounds over time. The Hosseinzadeh Bakhtouri study offers indicative support for a proposition that the operating reality of the sector already suggests. Marketing maturity in maritime firms is a property of how the organisation is led and how prepared it is to act, and those properties decide outcomes ahead of the size of the technology budget.
