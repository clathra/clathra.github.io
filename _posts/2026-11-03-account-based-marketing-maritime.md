In most maritime and offshore markets the set of realistic buyers is small and nameable. A supplier of a specialised retrofit, a P&I service, an offshore support vessel, or a niche brokerage is not selling to a mass market. It is selling to a few hundred owners, operators, charterers, or yards, most of which can be listed by name. This is an unusual position by the standards of general marketing, and it has clear implications for how the work should be organised.

The relevant framework is account-based marketing, set out in detail by Bacon in Seebacher's B2B Marketing Guidebook (2025). Account-based marketing reverses the usual sequence. It begins with a defined list of target accounts and concentrates marketing and sales effort on those specific organisations, and often on named individuals within them. Bacon describes three tiers: one-to-one programmes for a small number of strategic accounts, one-to-few programmes for clusters of accounts that share a context, and one-to-many programmes that apply the same logic at larger scale.

## Why it suits the maritime condition

The case for the approach is sharpest where the buyer universe is genuinely finite, which is the maritime situation. Andrea Clatworthy of Fujitsu, quoted in the same volume, described turning off conventional demand generation entirely without anyone noticing, because in a market of a defined set of large accounts the broad lead-generation machine produces mostly noise. The maritime equivalent is that a campaign measured in impressions across a global audience is largely wasted when the entire addressable market would fit in a single conference hall.

The three tiers map onto the sector cleanly. A one-to-one approach suits the handful of tier-one owners or operators whose recurring spend justifies bespoke attention, where the supplier can reasonably understand each account's fleet, renewal timing, and decision-makers. A one-to-few approach suits clusters that share a regulatory or operational situation, such as tanker owners facing a common vetting regime, offshore-wind service operators, or cruise lines, where the message is specific enough to be credible and general enough to be reused across the cluster. A one-to-many approach applies the same account focus to the broader population of technical superintendents, designated persons, and procurement staff who recur across the market.

## The four phases of the work

Bacon (2025) describes account-based marketing as a sequence of four phases. The first is to define the target accounts. The second is to identify the specific buyers within each account, since a purchase of any size in shipping involves several roles across the buying organisation. The third is to build content addressed to those buyers and their situation. The fourth is deployment, the act of putting that content in front of the named individuals through the appropriate channels. Bacon notes that the degree of alignment between sales and marketing rises across the sequence: the early definition work can sit largely with marketing, while deployment depends on sales and marketing operating from one shared view of each account. In a maritime supplier this matters because the commercial team usually owns the relationship and the marketing team usually owns the content, and the four phases force those two functions into a single account plan.

Account selection itself rests on two inputs. The first is an ideal-customer profile, a description of the attributes that make an account worth pursuing. The second is an account-opportunity view that ranks the candidate accounts by growth potential and likely return, so that the finite marketing budget is pointed at the accounts where it earns the most. For a one-to-one pilot, Bacon suggests a deliberately small starting set, typically around two to five accounts, which keeps the early work tractable and lets the supplier learn the mechanics before scaling to the wider tiers.

## What it asks for

The approach draws on something the maritime industry already possesses, which is detailed knowledge of who the customers are. Commercial and chartering teams carry this knowledge as a matter of course. Account-based marketing is, in part, the work of bringing the marketing function into the same view of the market that the commercial side already holds, so that both address the same named accounts with a consistent message and at the same scale.

The approach also demands patience, since it is a sustained, multi-touch effort. Ender, in the same volume, puts the figure at six to eight contacts to generate one qualified lead, which sets a realistic expectation for any supplier weighing the effort. A single well-produced piece of content sent once to a target account will rarely move it. The account-based logic accepts this and plans a programme of repeated, relevant contact across the buying group, spread over the months that a maritime purchase decision typically takes. Measured against that benchmark, the broad campaign that touches a global audience once is weaker still, because it spends its reach on accounts that will never buy and leaves the named accounts under-contacted.

The specific figures cited for account-based marketing in the general B2B literature, such as the proportion of marketers who rate it important, are borrowed from outside the sector and carry little weight here. The structural point is what transfers. When the addressable market is a finite list of named buyers, the efficient design of marketing is to work the list. Much maritime marketing effort is still spent on broad visibility, the trade-press advertisement and the general campaign, in markets small enough that the names of nearly all the buyers are already known. Directing that effort at the known accounts, through a defined set of phases and across enough contacts to register, is the more defensible use of it.
