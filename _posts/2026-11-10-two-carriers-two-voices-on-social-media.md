Maersk and Mediterranean Shipping Company sat at almost the same point in the container market in 2020. D'Agostini, in a 2023 study in the Maritime Business Review, records Maersk near 17.1 per cent of global market share and MSC near 16.0 per cent, with comparable fleet capacity. Two firms of similar size, competing for the same cargo, made an instructive pair for studying how a carrier communicates. The study mined the text of 277 Facebook posts, 131 from Maersk and 146 from MSC, published between May 2020 and February 2021, and mapped the engagement each one drew. The findings rest on one platform across a nine-month window for two companies, so they describe an indicative pattern that warrants caution. The pattern is sharp enough to read closely.

## Two content strategies, drawn from two business strategies

The most visible difference between the two feeds was subject matter. D'Agostini found that Maersk's posts ranged across logistics, supply chain coordination and intermodal services. The content tracked the firm's published strategy of vertical integration, in which the carrier sells end-to-end transport across the whole journey. The feed read as a description of that ambition, with cargo moving across modes and through inland networks.

MSC's feed was close to monothematic. The text mining returned "TEU" and "vessel" among its dominant terms, and the posts returned repeatedly to ships and to capacity. That focus mirrored MSC's own strategy at the time, built on aggressive fleet growth and scale at sea. Where Maersk described a system, MSC described its ships, and it did so with a consistency that left little doubt about what the brand stood for.

Each firm's content matched its commercial posture. The alignment is the first point worth holding: in both cases the public voice reflected the actual strategy of the business, so a reader of either feed could infer how the company intended to win. The two voices diverged because the two strategies diverged, and the feeds were honest reflections of each.

## The narrower, more consistent voice drew more engagement

The engagement figures ran against the assumption that broader content performs better. D'Agostini reports that MSC drew an average of 32.5 comments per post against Maersk's 12.9, and an average of 359.9 shares per post against Maersk's 61.4. The narrower feed, anchored to a single recognisable theme, outdrew the wider one by a wide margin on both measures. The study also found MSC's interaction network more centralised, with engagement concentrated around its core message.

The timing makes the gap harder to attribute to reach. MSC began publishing on the platform later than Maersk and arrived with a smaller established audience, yet it generated the higher response. Because the two carriers held near-identical market share and similar fleet capacity in the period studied, the engagement gap is a communication outcome. The difference lay in what each firm said and how consistently it said it.

Notteboom and colleagues, in a 2024 study in Maritime Economics and Logistics, extend the picture beyond Facebook. Drawing on 703 posts published between December 2020 and November 2022, the authors find MSC again posting a higher engagement rate per follower than Maersk across Facebook, Instagram and LinkedIn, despite Maersk's larger absolute following. The 2024 work rests on an expert panel of twelve and a single industry, so it too is indicative. Its agreement with the earlier study across three platforms and a later window strengthens the reading. The study also notes that MSC measured success by engagement rate, while the larger follower count belonged to the carrier drawing the lower rate.

## What the comparison implies for a maritime brand

The two carriers supply a controlled comparison that the sector rarely offers. Same market share, same scale, opposite content strategies, and a clear separation in engagement that favours the firm with the tighter theme. The lesson for a maritime brand is that a recognisable, consistent story carries the response, and that audience size is a weak proxy for the strength of that story. Maersk held the larger following and the broader content, and drew the lower engagement rate per follower in both studies.

The first discipline the comparison points to is alignment. Each carrier's content mirrored its real strategy, which is what made each feed legible. A brand that posts material disconnected from how the business actually competes forfeits that legibility, and the audience is left guessing what the firm is for. The content strategy should be a reading of the commercial strategy, so that the public voice and the actual business say the same thing.

The second discipline is commitment to a theme. MSC's gain came from returning to one subject often enough that the brand became identifiable from a single post. A feed that covers everything its owner does tends to be remembered for nothing in particular. For an operator, owner or offshore contractor choosing what to publish, the more useful question concerns which single capability the brand wants to own in the minds of charterers and counterparties, and then publishing against that capability with the consistency the MSC feed showed.

The third point concerns measurement. The carrier that judged itself on engagement rate ran the feed that performed. Follower totals reward early starts and large marketing budgets, and they say little about whether the content reaches the people who decide on charters and mandates. Engagement rate is the closer read on whether a brand's story is landing. Across two studies, two periods and several platforms, the firm with the clearer and more consistent voice drew the stronger response, and it did so while holding the smaller audience.
