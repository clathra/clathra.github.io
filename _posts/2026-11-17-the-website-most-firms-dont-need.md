The standard advice given to a small business setting up online is to commission a custom website, and the standard purchase that follows is a content-managed system. The Clutch State of Small Business Websites 2025 survey, an industry survey of small firms, found that 83 percent of small businesses now have a website, up from 64 percent in 2018. The composition of those sites is the interesting part. Clutch reports that 41 percent are built on DIY no-code builders, 34 percent run on WordPress or Shopify, and only 12 percent are custom-built. Most firms that succeed online do so without the bespoke build that the default advice points them toward, and the gap between what gets recommended and what gets used is wide.

The question worth asking is what a small B2B site is actually for. For a firm whose buyers are other businesses, the site does two jobs. It establishes credibility for a prospect who has heard the name and wants to check it is real and serious, and it is found when someone searches for the capability. A site that loads quickly, states plainly what the firm does, and proves it is a going concern has done the work. The features that a content-managed platform exists to provide, the dashboards, the plugins, the editorial workflows, sit outside that brief for most firms.

## The cost of the platform

The expense of a managed site falls into two parts, and the larger one is recurring. Leadpages, a vendor in this market, put the build cost of an agency-made small-business site at about 2,000 to 9,000 dollars in 2024, with average annual maintenance around 1,200 dollars and managed plans running 50 to 500 dollars a month. These are vendor figures and read accordingly, but the structure they describe is consistent across the sector. Codeable, another vendor, reported in 2026 that WordPress maintenance commonly runs 140 to 1,000 dollars or more a month. Liquid Web, a vendor figure from 2026, estimated that once labour is included, business-site spend often reaches 300 to 3,500 dollars a month.

The contrast with a static site is stark. GitHub Pages, per its official documentation, offers free static hosting for sites up to 1 GB, with around 100 GB of bandwidth a month, free SSL, and a custom domain at no charge. A small B2B firm sits well inside those limits. The ceiling on hosting cost for the kind of site such a firm needs is close to zero, which means almost the entire recurring spend on a managed site pays for the maintenance of the platform itself. The buyer of the firm's services sees none of it.

## Weight that accumulates

A content-managed site grows heavier over time, and the trend is measurable across the open web. The HTTP Archive Web Almanac 2025, a non-commercial study of millions of sites, reports that the median page now weighs 2,862 KB on desktop and 2,559 KB on mobile. The same source finds that the median mobile home page has grown about 202.8 percent over the decade, and that a median mobile home page ships around 632 KB of JavaScript and 911 KB of images. A managed platform tends toward this median because its plugins, themes, and tracking scripts each add weight, and removing them later is harder than adding them was.

The mechanism behind the growth has a name in software practice. Martin Fowler, in his 2015 essay "Yagni", set out that presumptive features carry four costs: the cost to build them, the cost of the delay they impose on shipping other work, and the carry and repair cost of maintaining them once they exist. Fowler cites evidence that only around a third of built features improved the metric they were meant to improve. A content-managed site is a standing collection of presumptive features. Most go unused by a small B2B firm, and each one continues to charge carry and repair against the budget whether or not it earns its place.

The performance difference shows up directly when two builds are compared. Shah and Joshi, writing in 2026 in the International Journal of Computer Applications, a lower-tier peer-reviewed journal whose result is best treated as indicative, ran two identical hotel websites head to head. The monolithic build used a browser heap of 17.7 MB against 7.0 MB for the static build, and the static version loaded faster because its pre-rendered assets came straight from a CDN. A single paired comparison proves little on its own, but the direction matches the page-weight evidence and the cost structure above.

## Where complexity earns its place

The case for a lean site is conditional, and the conditions matter. Complexity is genuinely warranted for transactional e-commerce, where a catalogue, a basket, and payment processing are the product. It is warranted for a large content team publishing constantly, where an editorial workflow saves more labour than it costs. And it is warranted where non-technical staff need to edit the site frequently, since a content management interface is the thing that lets them. A firm in any of those positions is buying a platform that does real work for it. The argument here is about the firm that buys the same platform and uses none of those capabilities.

For a shipping company, the reading follows from what the site has to accomplish. A maritime operator, a shipyard, or a port-services firm sells to a small number of professional buyers who already know the segment. The site exists to confirm that the firm is real, to state its fleet, yard, or service clearly, and to appear when a charterer or procurement team searches for the capability. A fast static site does that completely, and it does it without a recurring platform bill or a page that gains weight each year. The implication is to right-size the site to its real job. The credibility and findability that a B2B buyer needs are built through clear positioning, accurate information, and speed, and a firm that spends its web budget on a heavy platform it does not use has paid for carry and repair in place of the marketing work that would actually move a buyer.
