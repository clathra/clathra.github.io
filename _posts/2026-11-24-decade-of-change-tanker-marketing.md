Over the past twenty years the way shipping companies promote themselves has changed substantially, at least among the firms that promote themselves at all. The clearest record of this comes from the 2026 study by Plomaritou and Hadjiconstantinou in the WMU Journal of Maritime Affairs, which surveyed Greek tanker companies in 2005 and 2025 and compared the promotional methods they reported using.

The movement runs from a print and trade-fair model toward digital channels, content, and reputation.

## Three movements

The first is the near-universal adoption of digital promotion, from a marginal 5 percent of firms in 2005 to 87 percent in 2025. The second is the arrival of sustainability as a promotional theme, reported by 7 percent of firms at the start of the period and 85 percent at the end, which tracks the regulatory and charterer pressure that has built around emissions and environmental performance over the same period. The third is the appearance of employer branding, from 3 percent of firms to 65 percent, reflecting a crewing and shore-staff market in which standing as an employer carries commercial value. Plomaritou and Hadjiconstantinou (2026) record all three as among the steepest changes in the survey.

Print advertising moved the other way, from 33 percent of firms to 5 percent. Trade fairs and conferences held up well and grew, from 25 percent to 72 percent, which fits an industry that still does significant relationship work in person at events such as Posidonia and Nor-Shipping, while shifting the informational and reputational layer of its marketing online. The two readings together describe a sector that kept its physical meeting places and rebuilt everything around them.

## A communications function takes shape

Beneath the headline channel figures, Plomaritou and Hadjiconstantinou (2026) record a second pattern: the disciplines associated with a managed communications function all rose together. Relationship marketing, already the most common method in 2005 at 38 percent, reached 74 percent. Public relations climbed from 18 percent to 69 percent. Content marketing, reported by 9 percent of firms at the start, reached 69 percent. Corporate branding moved from 32 percent to 67 percent, and direct marketing, the one method that was already widespread in 2005 at 65 percent, edged up to 71 percent. Sponsorships rose from 10 percent to 62 percent.

The clustering matters more than any single line. Relationship marketing, public relations and corporate branding are the components of a reputation that is actively managed and resourced. Their parallel rise indicates that the firms in the survey came to treat external perception as something to be planned, resourced and measured. The growth of public relations in particular, from a fifth of firms to better than two thirds, points to companies that now expect to be written about, scrutinised by financiers and counterparties, and held to account on incidents, and that prepare for those moments in advance.

Corporate video and multimedia carries the same signal in sharper form, moving from 5 percent of firms to 68 percent. Producing video is a deliberate act. It requires a budget line, a brief, and a view about what the company wants understood about its vessels, its crews and its safety record. Its near-absence in 2005 and its presence across most firms in 2025 marks the point at which tanker marketing acquired a production capability, with the planning and spending that implies. Content marketing at 69 percent reinforces the reading: firms that publish regularly have committed to a sustained editorial rhythm, moving past the occasional announcement.

Taken together, these methods describe an industry professionalising its communications. The work that was once handled informally, through personal contact and the occasional brochure, has been organised into recognisable disciplines with owners, schedules and outputs. The same study notes that this professionalisation has not reached evenly across the fleet.

## What the record does and does not show

These figures describe the firms that market, and they sit alongside the same study's structural finding that most small owners still have no marketing function at all. The picture is of a capability that has modernised at the top of the market while remaining absent at the bottom. Large firms, carrying the reputational exposure of listed and oil-major counterparties, have built digital, video and sustainability communications that would have been unfamiliar twenty years ago. Smaller owners largely have not, and the gap between the two groups has widened as the leading firms have added whole disciplines on top of changing their channels.

That divide is the most consequential reading of the data. A method adopted by most surveyed firms still leaves a large population of owners for whom the same method is unused. The averages describe a direction of travel set by the companies with the resources to follow it, and they conceal a long tail that has stayed where it was in 2005.

For an owner considering where to begin, the value of this record is that it removes the guesswork about channels. The methods the more sophisticated firms have converged on are known: a credible website, a consistent presence on the professional networks where charterers and crews actually are, clear environmental reporting, produced video that explains the fleet and its standards, and the in-person relationship work the sector has always done. The channels are settled, and the disciplines that surround them are documented. What remains scarce, and therefore valuable, is doing them well and consistently over time.
