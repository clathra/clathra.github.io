The maritime industry conducts much of its commercial relationship-building in person, at a relatively small number of large events. Posidonia, Nor-Shipping, SMM, Gastech for the gas trades, and the segment conferences form the calendar around which a great deal of business development is organised. The assumption embedded in that calendar is that decisions are made in the room. The evidence from how businesses buy suggests they are increasingly made well before it.

General research on B2B purchasing, summarised in Seebacher's B2B Marketing Guidebook (2025), reports that business buyers commonly complete a majority of their purchasing process before contacting a vendor, with a figure of around 57 percent widely cited. The same body of work finds that buyers run roughly a dozen independent online searches before they reach out, and that most industrial purchases, around two thirds, are influenced by digital channels. These figures describe general B2B behaviour across industries, and they apply to the maritime market only by extension. They vary by source and are best treated as indicative. The underlying pattern is what matters. By the time a buyer initiates contact, a good deal of evaluation has already happened, conducted privately and online.

The same general B2B evidence, drawing on Ender in the same volume, notes that it commonly takes six to eight contacts to generate a qualified lead. Read alongside the earlier figures, the implication is consistent. A buyer who has already completed most of an evaluation alone is a buyer who has chosen, from many candidates, the few worth the repeated contact that a qualified relationship requires. The selection that narrows the field happens during the private phase, before a salesperson is aware that an evaluation is under way.

## No reason maritime is exempt

A technical superintendent sourcing a ballast-water system, a charterer forming a view of an unfamiliar operator, a yard buyer comparing suppliers for a newbuild package, an offshore project manager drawing up a bid list, all have access to the same search engines, professional networks, and public records as any other industrial buyer, and all use them. The work these buyers do is specialised, and the way they begin it is ordinary. A specification is checked against what suppliers publish, a counterparty's reputation is assembled from public traces, a shortlist is drawn from the names that surface and read credibly. By the time the meeting at the stand takes place, the candidate has usually already been read: its website, its people on professional networks, its public track record, its presence or absence in the places a buyer looks.

This shapes what the trade show is for. The event remains valuable for the relationship work the industry does well, and the meeting is increasingly the confirmation of a shortlist assembled online in the preceding months. A company that is hard to find, or that presents poorly when found, is filtered out before it has the chance to make its case in person. An expensive stand cannot recover a candidate the buyer's earlier research has already set aside.

## The online presence as qualifier

The argument follows from the sequence. If a majority of the evaluation is complete before contact, and if the candidates worth the six to eight contacts of a qualified relationship are chosen during that private phase, then the online presence performs the qualifying function. It decides who is read seriously, who survives the comparison, and who is offered the meeting that the calendar is built around. The website, the professional-network profiles, and the public evidence of completed work operate as the entry test for the in-person stage. A presence that is thin, dated, or absent answers that test in the negative before any conversation begins.

For a technical buyer, the presence is read for competence: whether the supplier demonstrably understands the system, the regulation, and the operating reality. For a charterer or a yard buyer, it is read for credibility and for evidence that the counterparty has done the work before. The standard is set by the buyer's search, conducted on the buyer's terms, and a candidate is measured against it in absence. The qualification has happened by the time anyone walks the hall.

## The practical reading

The in-person work the sector prizes now depends on an online layer that precedes it. The website, the professional-network presence, and the public evidence of competence determine whether the relationship at the stand ever begins. For an industry that has invested heavily in the events and lightly in everything around them, the imbalance is worth noticing. The handshake still matters, and the research that decides who is offered one now happens somewhere else, in the weeks and months before anyone arrives at the hall.
