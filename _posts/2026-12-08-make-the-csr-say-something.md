Corporate social responsibility has become a standing fixture in how shipping firms present themselves online. Šekularac-Ivošević and colleagues (2023), writing in STED Journal, studied this directly through a content analysis of shipping firms' social media. For the ship manager they examined, corporate social responsibility was the single largest content category. The volume signals that the sector treats responsibility as something audiences want to see. The composition of that volume is where the finding gets interesting.

## What the content category actually contains

The study found that most of the corporate social responsibility material was generic. It consisted of holiday and maritime-day greetings, employee health and vaccination notices, and broad mentions of charity, ecology, and human rights. Substantive and specific material was a small share of the whole. A category that dominates by quantity was thin in detail. The firm posted often under the responsibility banner while saying little that a reader could weigh, check, or use to distinguish one operator from another.

The sample is small, so the breakdown should be read as indicative. It points to a pattern worth testing against a wider set of firms before it is taken as a settled measurement. Even as an indication, it aligns with what a casual scan of maritime social feeds tends to surface, where commemorative posts and general statements of good intent are common and granular evidence is scarce.

## The expectation has formed, the supply has not

Maritime audiences, including charterers and the wider trade, now expect sustainability and responsibility signals as a matter of course. The expectation is established. The supply that meets it is mostly filler that differentiates nothing. When every firm posts the same maritime-day greeting and the same general commitment to the environment, the content cancels out across the field. A reader scrolling through it gains no basis for preferring one operator over the next, because the signals carry no specific information.

Concrete proof is comparatively rare. Specific decarbonisation steps, measured safety performance, and real community or training commitments appear far less often than generic gestures. The Šekularac-Ivošević and colleagues (2023) finding frames this gap precisely. The high-volume category is dominated by material that resists verification, and the verifiable material sits at the margins. That imbalance describes open ground.

## Why the open ground is cheap to take

The economics of differentiation usually run the other way. Standing apart tends to cost more, because rivals are already competing hard on the same dimension. Here the dimension is contested in volume and neglected in substance. A firm that publishes substantive, verifiable sustainability content stands apart in a field of greetings. Because the bar that competitors have set is low, the cost of clearing it is modest. The raw material already exists inside most operators in the form of fleet measures, audit results, safety records, and training programmes. The work amounts to disclosure and presentation, since the substance is already on hand.

A specific decarbonisation step stated with figures and dates says more than a year of ecology mentions. A safety record reported with its method and its numbers carries weight that a vaccination notice cannot. Community and training commitments described with names, places, and durations read as facts a reader can confirm. Each of these clears the genre's prevailing standard at low cost, precisely because the prevailing standard is greeting-grade.

## The commercial weight the content has to carry

Environmental credentials increasingly affect who wins cargo. Charterers screen on emissions profiles, regulatory readiness, and demonstrated performance, and these factors feed into commercial decisions about which operators to use. Sustainability content therefore has a buyer, and that buyer acts on specifics. A charterer cannot price a holiday greeting into a fixture. A charterer can act on a measured efficiency figure, a documented compliance status, or a verifiable safety statistic.

This reframes the content question. The point of substantive sustainability material is to give a commercial audience something it can take into a decision. Content that meets the formed expectation with filler spends attention and returns nothing. Content built on concrete, checkable proof meets the same expectation and supplies a decision input at the same time. Given a field where the Šekularac-Ivošević and colleagues (2023) analysis indicates that filler dominates the largest category, the operator that supplies proof occupies ground that is both visible and largely unclaimed.
