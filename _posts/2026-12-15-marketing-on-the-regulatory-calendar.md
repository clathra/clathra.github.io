Shipping runs on a dense and public schedule of regulation. The IMO 2020 sulphur cap, the EEXI and CII efficiency measures that took effect in 2023, the extension of the EU Emissions Trading System to shipping from 2024, FuelEU Maritime from 2025, and the Ballast Water Management Convention, whose phased compliance completed by 2024, each arrived on a known timetable, and each forced a wave of decisions across owners, operators, and their suppliers. This calendar is, among other things, a marketing asset, because it is a reliable guide to the questions the industry's buyers are actively trying to answer.

The sequence rewards attention as a sequence. The sulphur cap of 2020 settled questions of fuel choice and exhaust treatment. The EEXI and CII measures of 2023 turned the focus to design efficiency and operational carbon intensity. The extension of the EU Emissions Trading System to shipping from 2024 attached a direct cost to emitted carbon, and FuelEU Maritime from 2025 added a standard for the greenhouse-gas intensity of energy used on board. The Ballast Water Management Convention, with phased compliance completed by 2024, ran in parallel on a separate track. Each measure builds on the conditions left by the ones before it, so the questions a buyer asks in one year are shaped by the rule that landed in the last.

The underlying principle is set out by Mörk in Seebacher's B2B Marketing Guidebook (2025) under the heading of situational content marketing. The argument is that the relevance of content rises when it attaches to a buyer's live situation, and that legal and regulatory change is one of the strongest such triggers, because compliance is mandatory and the deadline is fixed. A buyer who can defer most reading cannot defer a rule that carries a fixed date and a binding obligation. Content that helps that buyer understand and act on an impending rule reaches an audience that is, for once, looking for exactly that information, at the moment the search is most active.

## Clear triggers in a maritime setting

When a fuel or emissions rule approaches, technical superintendents, designated persons, and commercial and technical managers begin actively searching for clear explanations of what it requires, what the options are, and what it will cost. These are the people who carry the consequence of the rule into the fleet. The technical superintendent translates the requirement into work on the vessel, the designated person holds responsibility for the safety and compliance link between ship and shore, and the commercial and technical managers weigh the cost against the trading position of the ship. Each reads with a specific decision in view.

A supplier whose service touches that rule, a fuel-additive maker, a scrubber or ballast-system vendor, a classification society, a software provider, an advisory firm, has a natural and timely subject to write about, addressed to an audience motivated to read it. The same supplier's general capability messaging, published without that hook, competes for attention the buyer is not currently giving. The difference lies in timing as much as in subject. Material on carbon intensity carried weight as the CII measures took effect in 2023, and material on the cost of emitted carbon found a ready audience as the Emissions Trading System reached shipping from 2024. The measure sets the moment, and the moment determines whether the content is read.

## The discipline it requires

The work demands accuracy and restraint. Regulation is read by a technical and skeptical audience that will notice an explanation bent to flatter a particular product. A misread threshold, a compliance date stated wrong, or a requirement softened to fit a product's strengths registers immediately with readers who hold the regulation open while they read. The value of regulatory content comes from being genuinely useful and correct about the rule itself, which builds the standing that makes the supplier worth approaching when the buyer acts. Content that uses the regulation as a pretext and pivots quickly to a sales message forfeits that standing, and forfeits it with the precise audience the supplier most wants to reach.

The practical attraction of the regulatory calendar is its predictability. The deadlines are published years ahead, the sequence of measures is known, and the questions each one raises are foreseeable. A maritime supplier can plan a year of genuinely useful, well-timed material around it without guessing at what its audience will care about, because the regulator has already decided. The calendar also supplies its own cadence. Each effective date marks a point where attention concentrates, and the months ahead of it mark the window when explanation is most wanted. Among the available subjects for maritime marketing, the next rule change is consistently the one the audience has the strongest reason to read about.
