A container terminal earns most of its revenue from a small number of carriers. When one of those carriers moves its cargo elsewhere, the loss is concentrated and immediate. Notteboom has estimated that a single line ceasing to berth can cost a port roughly 20 percent of its throughput. That concentration is what makes the question of customer retention central to port economics, and it is the question Caliskan and Esmer take up in their 2019 study in Case Studies on Transport Policy, "Does it really worth investing in relationship marketing for a port business?"

The study surveyed all 24 Turkish container terminals and collected 134 manager-level responses. The sample is small and the measures are self-reported, so the figures are best read as indicative of how port managers perceive the mechanics of their own customer relationships, with limited claim to precision as population estimates. Within those limits, the pattern the authors trace is consistent and worth examining, because it points to where the value in a port relationship actually sits and how little of it is visible to the carriers a port has not yet won.

## What relationship marketing buys a port

Caliskan and Esmer build on Berry's three levels of relationship marketing. Financial relationship marketing covers price-based ties such as discounts and deferred payment terms. Social relationship marketing covers the human side of the account: personal contact, complaint handling, and face-to-face relationships. Structural relationship marketing covers the operational bonds that are hard to replicate, including dedicated terminals, long-term contracts, shared electronic data interchange systems, and services tailored to a specific carrier.

The authors model a chain that runs from these marketing activities through relationship quality, then to customer-relationship performance, and finally to financial performance. The path coefficients tell a clear story about which level of effort matters. Structural relationship marketing had the strongest link to relationship quality, with a standardized coefficient around 0.76 to 0.78. Financial relationship marketing came next at roughly 0.53 to 0.58, and social relationship marketing trailed at about 0.37 to 0.43. Relationship quality then carried into customer-relationship performance at around 0.65 to 0.71, and that performance carried into financial performance at about 0.73 to 0.79.

The ordering is the finding. Discounts and friendly account management both register, but the operational architecture of the relationship, the dedicated capacity and the integrated systems and the tailored service, is what the data ties most tightly to the quality of the relationship that eventually shows up in the accounts. One caveat deserves to be stated plainly: the direct path from relationship quality to financial performance was significant at the within-port level but was not verified at the between-firm level. The chain holds inside the cases the authors studied; the authors stop short of claiming it as a verified cross-firm law.

## Why retention is the harder economic problem

The reason these relationships carry weight is that the alternative, replacing a lost carrier, is expensive and uncertain. Retaining an existing customer costs less than winning a new one, a regularity the authors note holds across service industries, and the cost of switching in container shipping is high enough that price alone moves volumes. Caliskan and Esmer cite cases that make the point concrete.

A rise of roughly 10 percent in port charges at Hamburg and Bremen reduced the number of carrier calls those ports received. Maersk moved its operations away from Singapore's PSA to Tanjung Pelepas over a price difference of about 30 percent, and PSA went on to lose around 11 percent of its business, with Evergreen and APL departing in later periods. These are not marginal adjustments. A carrier deciding where to call is weighing a large recurring cost against the friction of moving, and when the price gap grows wide enough, the friction loses.

That dynamic explains why non-price competition is rising in importance among ports. A terminal cannot always win on tariff, and matching a 30 percent discount is rarely viable. What it can do is raise the cost of leaving by deepening the structural relationship: integrating systems, committing capacity, and tailoring services so that a carrier's operation is built partly around the terminal. The Caliskan and Esmer coefficients suggest this is also the most productive use of relationship effort, since structural ties showed the strongest connection to relationship quality. The economics of retention and the mechanics of relationship quality point in the same direction.

## The signal a port fails to send

The complication for a port is that the asset doing the work is hard to see. Relationship quality lives inside an existing account. The carrier that already berths at a terminal knows how its complaints are handled, how its dedicated capacity performs, and how quickly the terminal adapts when a schedule changes. A prospective carrier knows none of this. From the outside, two terminals with very different records on responsiveness and tailoring can look identical, distinguishable only by published tariffs. The non-price strength that the data identifies as decisive is precisely the strength that does not announce itself.

This is where a port's brand and content do real economic work for Clathra's clients. Structural relationship marketing builds the relationship quality that the Caliskan and Esmer chain ties to financial performance, but that quality is invisible to the next prospective customer until the port makes its partnership posture and track record visible. A port that has integrated systems with its lines, met service commitments through disruption, and resolved problems without losing volume holds exactly the evidence a cautious carrier wants before committing. Surfacing that evidence, through case material on how services were tailored, how a difficult call was handled, and how long the major relationships have held, converts an internal operational strength into an external signal. That signal is what retains the carriers already berthing and what gives a prospective line a reason to weigh the relationship against a competitor's lower tariff.

The Turkish terminal data is indicative and bounded, and the authors are careful about what it does and does not establish across firms. Read alongside the switching cases, though, it sketches a coherent account of port competition in which the relationships a terminal has built are its most defensible asset and, left unspoken, its most underused one.
