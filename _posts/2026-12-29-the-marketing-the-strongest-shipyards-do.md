Shipbuilding rarely appears in conversations about marketing. The order is large, the buyer is sophisticated, and the relationship between yard and owner is measured in years of correspondence before a contract is signed. The assumption that follows is that promotion has little to do with where the orders land. A 2022 study of the international shipbuilding industry tested that assumption directly and found a measurable link between the marketing a yard puts on public display and how its orderbook performs.

## What the study measured, and how

Mascaraque-Ramirez and Para-Gonzalez, writing in the Proceedings of the Institution of Mechanical Engineers Part M: Journal of Engineering for the Maritime Environment in 2022, asked whether the six standard dimensions of marketing promotion improve performance in shipbuilding. The six dimensions are personal sale, advertising, sales promotion, sponsorship, public relations, and direct marketing.

Their method matters for how the result should be read. The authors built a Marketing Promotion Index, scored from 0 to 1, by reading what yards themselves published: annual reports and corporate websites. The index therefore captures the marketing a yard makes publicly visible, the part of the promotional effort that any owner, competitor, or analyst can see without an introduction. This is a method built on public reports. The figures that follow are indicative of that grey-literature source, dated and self-published, and should be read with the caution due such material.

The world index came out at roughly 0.40. Regional spread was wide. European yards scored highest at 0.52. The grouping the authors label Rest of Asia, which includes China, sat lowest at 0.25. A gap of that size between regions is the first signal that promotional effort in shipbuilding is uneven, which makes it a variable in play across the industry.

## Where the effort goes, and where it does not

The per-dimension averages give the index its shape. Personal sale scored close to 1.00 across the industry, meaning every yard maintains the direct, relationship-led selling that has always defined the trade. That dimension is universal, so it cannot distinguish one yard from another.

The other five dimensions tell a more useful story. Public relations averaged about 0.36 across the world's yards, direct marketing about 0.37, advertising about 0.31, and sales promotion about 0.33. Sponsorship sat almost empty at roughly 0.03, a dimension the industry has left essentially unused.

The performance link the authors report is specific. Advertising, public relations, and direct marketing varied meaningfully from yard to yard, and that variation tracked performance. Yards in Japan and Korea that put greater effort into these dimensions showed stronger orderbooks and faster new-order growth than their Rest-of-Asia rivals. The three dimensions that stayed flat across the industry, personal sale, sales promotion, and sponsorship, did not separate the field. The authors conclude that advertising, public relations, and direct marketing carry a positive relationship with performance, while sponsorship and sales promotion remain under-exploited.

Two findings sit together here. The dimensions that distinguished the stronger yards were the ones the study could read from the outside, and one of the visible dimensions, sponsorship, is almost entirely vacant. A channel that correlates with nothing because nobody uses it is a different proposition from a channel that has been tried and found wanting.

## What the market actually pays for

The promotion finding gains force when it is set against an older observation about how shipbuilding prices form. Stott, in a 1995 paper for the SNAME Ship Production Symposium, argued that newbuild price moves on a commodity basis. An owner will not pay above the prevailing market price for a more sophisticated product, because the performance attributes of a design do not command a premium in the price. What design quality affects is the probability of a sale, the chance that a given yard wins the order at the market rate. That paper is dated and its market conditions belong to its decade, so it functions as an illustrative anchor, indicative of the period and not offered as current evidence.

Stott also held that a yard can no longer attempt to serve every owner, and that success comes from matching a yard's facilities to a defined segment. The two ideas combine cleanly. If price is set by the market and the variable a yard controls is the probability of winning the order, then the work that raises that probability becomes the work that pays. Targeting a segment is one half of that work. Being legible to the segment is the other.

## The opening for an offshore-structures or newbuild yard

The 2022 index was assembled by reading yards' websites and annual reports, and the yards with more to show in those documents performed better. That sequence is worth stating plainly: the measurement instrument was the public record, and the public record predicted the orderbook. The promotional dimensions that separated the stronger performers were advertising, public relations, and direct marketing, all of which live in material an owner can find and read.

For a yard building newbuilds or offshore structures, this reframes a common posture. A yard that prefers to let the work speak for itself, declining to advertise, to publish, or to maintain an active public record, is choosing to leave empty the precise dimensions that the strongest performers populate. The orderbook is influenced by the probability of being chosen, and a yard that cannot be readily examined is harder to choose with confidence. Sponsorship compounds the point. It is the one visible dimension the entire industry has left vacant, which makes it an open lane available to any yard willing to be the first to occupy it.

The marketing footprint of a yard is its website, its published technical material, its press presence, and the readable trail of its delivered work. The Mascaraque-Ramirez and Para-Gonzalez study indicates that this footprint correlates with order performance, and that several of its dimensions remain thinly worked across the industry. The neglected dimensions are the opening. A visible, readable public record is the marketing the market rewards, and the yards that already understand this are the ones the index found at the top.
