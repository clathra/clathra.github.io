The trade show holds an unusual place in B2B marketing. It commands a large share of the budget, it concentrates the calendar around a few dates, and it carries the assumption that the deals are done in the hall. Research on event lead generation in the B2B sector questions that assumption at its core. A master's thesis by Shi (2023) at Haaga-Helia University of Applied Sciences, studying the case of Konecranes Port Solutions, a maker of container, terminal, and shipyard cranes and the software that runs them, sets out the gap between what an exhibitor spends to be present and what it does with the leads it collects. The case is a single qualitative study with seven interviewees, so its own findings are indicative; the load-bearing figures are industry numbers it cites, and they are best read the same way.

The scale of the commitment is the starting point. Trade shows often absorb more than half of a B2B firm's total marketing budget (Cortez and Brown, cited in Shi 2023). Against that outlay, the same work reports that more than 70 percent of exhibitors set no specific goals for the event, and that around 45 percent of B2B marketers struggle to justify what they spend on trade shows at all. A line item that consumes the majority of the budget is, for a large share of firms, run without a defined objective and defended with difficulty afterward. The expense is treated as a fixed cost of being in the market, and the return on it is left largely unexamined.

## The leads collected are mostly not ready

What the show actually produces is a stack of contacts at very different stages. Of the leads captured at a trade show, roughly 5 percent are hot and ready to buy, around 20 percent are sales-qualified but early in the funnel, and about 40 percent are marketing-qualified and need nurturing before they go anywhere (Gillett, cited in Shi 2023). The small group ready to act now is the exception. The majority of the value sitting in the badge scans and stand conversations is latent, attached to buyers who have signalled interest and are months or years from a decision.

That distribution sits inside a long timeline. Shi (2023) describes the value window of a trade show as running from roughly one year before the event to about two years after it, which places the show itself near the halfway point of the process. The hall is the midpoint. Most of the buyers a firm meets there will make their decision in the period that follows, and the contact at the stand is one input into a sequence that continues after the lights are switched off.

The maritime and port-equipment context stretches that sequence further. There are only around 1,500 to 2,000 ports and terminals worldwide, the buying population for this class of equipment is correspondingly small, and a single port crane can cost more than one million euros and be purchased perhaps once a decade (Shi 2023). Decisions run on multi-year cycles and pass through a decision-making unit of initiators, influencers, gatekeepers, users, buyers, and deciders, each of whom may form a view at a different moment. A buyer who is marketing-qualified at a show in one year is acting normally within this market when the order arrives three years later. The 5 percent ready to buy is a snapshot of a market where readiness is rare and patience is the default condition.

## The follow-up is where the value is kept or lost

If the majority of leads need nurturing across a window measured in years, the system that does the nurturing decides what the show was worth. The evidence on follow-up speed is unflattering. Online leads decay quickly, yet only 37 percent of companies follow up within an hour, and the average response time runs to around 42 hours (cited in Shi 2023). The contact most likely to convert is the one answered while interest is fresh, and the typical firm answers nearly two days later, by which point a fast-moving lead has cooled. The same lapse that wastes an online enquiry wastes a stand conversation, multiplied across hundreds of contacts gathered in a few days.

The mechanism Shi (2023) names for holding that value is owned digital infrastructure. Gated content on the firm's own website, whitepapers, recorded sessions, and online product-advisor tools, is the named method for converting anonymous traffic into identified leads and for keeping a relationship alive between the show and the decision. The website is where an interested but unready buyer can be given a reason to identify themselves, can be supplied with the technical material a long evaluation requires, and can be tracked and re-engaged as the buying cycle progresses. Without that backbone, the contacts from the hall sit in a list with no system around them, and the firm has no structured way to stay present during the two-year window when most of the decisions are actually made.

## The marketing reading for maritime exhibitors

The pattern the thesis describes maps onto how the maritime sector spends. The industry concentrates its marketing on a small number of large events and treats the stand as the destination, while only about 5 percent of the leads gathered there are ready to act. The other 95 percent are the asset, and they are an asset only to a firm equipped to capture and develop them after everyone has gone home. A booth without a web, content, and follow-up system behind it produces contacts that decay before they convert, which means more than half the marketing budget, the share the show consumes, largely evaporates once the event closes.

The distinction worth holding is between the buyer researching before the show and the buyer met during it. The pre-show buyer has been narrowing a shortlist online for months, and a credible presence is what earns the meeting. The buyer captured at the stand is a different problem, sitting at the midpoint of a years-long process and needing a reason to stay in contact. Both depend on the same owned digital layer. A website that hosts gated technical material, a content programme that gives an unready buyer a reason to return, and a follow-up process that responds while interest holds are what convert the expense of being present into pipeline that materialises later. For a sector that has invested heavily in the events and lightly in the systems around them, the figures point to where the unrealised value sits. The show ends at the halfway mark, and the half that decides the outcome is the part most exhibitors have built the least to handle.
