The market for tanker tonnage does not clear on price the way a commodity market does. Charterers in the oil and chemical trades are dominated by a small number of major and state oil companies, and the cargo they move carries environmental, legal, and reputational consequences that a freight saving cannot offset. The buying process is therefore organised around safety and reliability, and a few dollars per tonne matter far less than the confidence that a vessel and its operator will not become a problem.

This is well documented. Plomaritou and Menelaou, in 2020, set out a segmentation of shipping demand by the buying behaviour of the charterer: tanker charterers are safety-oriented, dry bulk charterers cost-oriented, and liner shippers quality-oriented. The distinction matters because each orientation directs attention to a different set of attributes. A safety-oriented charterer reads a service through the lens of risk, treating the avoidance of incident as the first condition of value. The segmentation determines which attributes of a service a marketer should make visible, and which carry little weight. For a tanker owner the implication is direct: the attributes that move a fixture are operational, and the attributes that a consumer marketer would reach for carry little weight with the people who control the cargo.

## The apparatus of trust

In the tanker trade that apparatus is formal. Major charterers operate vetting and approval systems, inspect vessels, and maintain lists of operators they will and will not use. Acceptance onto those lists is a precondition of access to the better cargoes. Oil-major charterers run their own inspection regimes alongside industry-wide schemes: OCIMF's SIRE inspections cover oil tankers, and CDI inspections cover chemical tankers, each producing a documented inspection report that other charterers consult. A clean record under these regimes opens the approved-operator lists; a poor one closes them. A tanker owner's commercial standing therefore rests on a documented record of safety, inspection outcomes, environmental compliance, and crew competence. The marketing task is largely the work of making that record legible and credible to the people who hold the approvals.

The 2026 survey of Greek tanker companies by Plomaritou and Hadjiconstantinou bears this out in the way firms allocate their effort. Every company surveyed used what the authors term physical-evidence strategies, tied to vessel performance and safety compliance, the highest score of any element in their framework. Only about a third used price-based strategies at all. In a market where the product is a safe, well-run ship, the marketing follows the same logic.

## A vocabulary for the work

The same body of work offers a structure for it. Plomaritou's framework of the eight Ps of the shipping marketing mix, first set out in 2008, extends the familiar marketing mix to the realities of a shipping service: the transport service itself, freight and hire as price, the chartering process, people and crew, ports as place, promotion, the physical evidence of the vessel, and paperless trade for the documentation and data that now accompany a fixture. For a tanker owner the weight of that mix sits on physical evidence, people, and process, the elements a safety-minded charterer scrutinises.

The 2026 survey shows how unevenly that weight is distributed in practice. Physical evidence, tied to safety and vessel performance, was applied by every firm, at 100 percent. The transport service followed at 90 percent and paperless trade at 82 percent. The voyage and post-fixture process registered 72 percent, people and crew 70 percent, and promotion 69 percent. The chartering process sat at 53 percent and place at 44 percent. Price, at 35 percent, was the element firms reached for least. The ordering inverts the priorities of a price-led market and confirms the segmentation: where the charterer buys safety, the marketing concentrates on the vessel itself and the documentation that vouches for it, and freight becomes a qualifying condition once the safety evidence has done the work of winning the cargo.

The survey also records movement over time. Comparing 2005 with 2025, Plomaritou and Hadjiconstantinou find that differentiation through personnel and crew rose from 21 to 43 percent of firms, roughly doubling, and differentiation through image rose from 59 to 65 percent. Both trends point the same way. As the technical specification of modern tonnage converges and inspection regimes tighten, the room to distinguish one operator from another narrows to the people who run the ships and the reputation the firm carries into a vetting room. Crew competence and corporate image are harder to copy than a hull, and the market has been pricing that scarcity for two decades.

## The practical reading

Effective marketing for a tanker owner looks unlike consumer marketing and unlike much of general B2B. Its raw material is operational: inspection records, safety statistics, fleet age and maintenance, officer retention, environmental performance, and the systems that govern them. Its audience is narrow and expert, and it rewards precision over persuasion. A claim that cannot survive a vetting inspector's scrutiny is worse than no claim at all, because the same audience that reads the marketing also holds the approvals. The work is closer to assembling a body of evidence than to crafting a message, and its credibility depends on the same records that determine whether a vessel clears its next inspection.

For owners in the dry bulk or liner trades the emphasis shifts. Dry bulk charterers buy on cost discipline, so the evidence that matters is efficiency, voyage economics, and the reliability of laytime and performance. Liner shippers buy on consistency and service quality, so the evidence is schedule integrity, coverage, and the dependability of repeated service. The orientation differs in each case, yet the underlying lesson holds across segments. The first task of marketing in shipping is to establish what the specific charterer is actually buying, and then to make the evidence of it plain.
