Port marketing was once a marginal activity, treated as something a public authority did reluctantly between the work of building quays and dredging channels. That has changed. In an editorial introducing a 2019 collection in Transport Policy, Parola, Pallis and Song survey the field and report that promotion has become routine across the sector. An ESPO survey from 2016, cited in that editorial, found that 81 percent of European port authorities now lead promotion and marketing activities themselves. When four in five public port bodies run their own marketing, the activity has stopped being a differentiator. The question for any maritime or offshore organisation is what separates effective marketing from the rest once almost everyone is doing it.

The same editorial assembles several findings that point to a consistent answer. The advantage has moved away from the act of promoting and toward the credibility of what is promoted, the precision of who it reaches, and the substance behind the claims. Each of the studies the editorial draws on illustrates one part of that shift.

## A crowded field with a thin theoretical base

The volume of activity is not matched by depth. A lexicometric review by Mandjak and colleagues, summarised in the same editorial, counted 369 port-marketing publications produced over forty years. Despite that output, the review concluded that port marketing still lacks a strong theoretical foundation and that the discipline is at its core business-to-business and relational in character. The buyers are carriers, shippers, terminal operators and industrial tenants, and the purchasing decisions are technical, repeated and built on long relationships.

That conclusion matters for how marketing should be designed. A relational, B2B market does not respond to consumer-style promotion. A shipper choosing where to route cargo, or an offshore operator selecting a fabrication yard, is evaluating berth productivity, hinterland connections, reliability and total cost. Promotion aimed at that audience competes on the quality of information it carries. The field has produced a large body of writing without a settled framework, which leaves a gap for organisations that can communicate with technical precision where generic claims of excellence prevail.

## Talking about green without showing it

The clearest illustration of the credibility gap comes from environmental positioning. A study of green-port practice by Lam and Li, covering thirty port cases and cited in the editorial, found that more than half of the ports examined engaged in green marketing. Yet the same study observed a recurring pattern in which ports tended to talk without doing. The strategy documents and public claims about environmental performance ran ahead of measurable evidence that the performance existed.

This is the central weakness in a market where promotion has become universal. When every port and every yard claims environmental progress, the claim itself conveys nothing. Audiences in a B2B setting are equipped to test assertions, and a regulator, a charterer or a cargo owner with sustainability obligations of their own will look for the underlying data. Claims that cannot be backed by figures degrade the credibility of the organisation making them. The marketing that survives scrutiny in this environment is the marketing that publishes the evidence alongside the claim: emissions measured, throughput verified, downtime recorded. The talk-without-doing pattern that Lam and Li identified is a marketing liability, because the gap between statement and proof is visible to the people the statement is meant to persuade.

## Loud promotion can attract the wrong cargo

Volume of promotion carries a second hazard, and it concerns who responds. Castelein and colleagues, studying the Rotterdam and Hamburg-Le Havre range and cited in the editorial, found that a port's attractiveness to carriers is a different thing from its attractiveness to shippers. The port that offered the most generous incentives to carriers disproportionately drew low-value activity, including empty-container handling and inefficient calls. The promotion worked, in that it pulled traffic, but it pulled the traffic that contributed least.

The lesson generalises beyond container ports. Promotion that is broad and undirected attracts whoever is cheapest to attract, and that is rarely the high-value customer. An offshore services provider that markets on price and incentives may fill capacity with marginal work while the contracts that justify the asset base go elsewhere. Targeting the right audience is therefore an economic decision with consequences for revenue quality. Marketing designed to reach a specific class of high-value buyer, with content built for that buyer's decision criteria, protects the quality of the business it generates. Marketing designed only to maximise response volume risks the outcome Castelein and colleagues documented.

## The barrier and the opening

The editorial names the obstacle directly. Parola, Pallis and Song describe a sector whose practitioners are auto-referential and conservative, working within a traditional culture of secrecy. That culture explains why the field can produce 369 publications and still lack a strong foundation, and why so many environmental claims stop at assertion. An industry that does not share data, that talks to itself and treats disclosure as risk, produces marketing that asserts much and demonstrates little.

The culture of secrecy is also the opening. The findings collected in the 2019 editorial converge on a single conclusion about maritime and port marketing as it now stands. Promotion is no longer scarce, so having a message confers no advantage. The advantage belongs to organisations that can prove what they claim with measurable evidence, that direct their communication at the specific buyers whose business is worth winning, and that compete on substance over the volume of their output. For a maritime and offshore marketing practice, this is the operative analysis. In a sector that has embraced marketing wholesale, evidence-backed content reaching the right audience outperforms louder promotion, and the firms willing to break from the inherited culture of secrecy are positioned to lead it.
