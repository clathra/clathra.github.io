Maritime firms address several distinct audiences at once. Owners and charterers make commercial decisions. Officers and ratings decide where to work and whether to stay. Ports, suppliers and the trade press form the surrounding market. These groups differ in age, profession and daily routine, and they occupy different digital spaces. A communication plan that treats them as one audience, reached through one channel, reaches each of them poorly.

## What the platforms actually carry

Research by Šekularac-Ivošević and colleagues, published in 2023 in STED Journal, examined how maritime organisations use social media and drew on a set of earlier studies. The picture that emerges is one of specialisation. Platforms carry different roles, and each serves a particular purpose within the communication mix.

In the documented practice of one container line, each platform held a defined function. Facebook carried visual and conversational content. Twitter carried business news. Instagram carried more entertaining content. LinkedIn was used to attract clients. The same organisation ran four channels with four purposes, and the content shifted to suit the medium and the people on it.

That allocation matters because the people on each platform are not the same. The study notes that engagement was rising on Instagram, a movement tied to newer generations of seafaring personnel. Younger officers and ratings gather where the content is lighter and more visual, and their presence there grows as older cohorts leave the workforce. The platform that reaches a master near retirement and the platform that reaches a cadet are unlikely to be identical.

## Where the shore-side market sits

The pattern holds for organisations as well as individuals. For port businesses, the research cited by Šekularac-Ivošević and colleagues found Twitter to be the dominant platform, followed by LinkedIn. For container ports specifically, LinkedIn was the most-preferred platform. Port authorities, terminal operators and their commercial counterparts congregate on professional networks, where industry news circulates and business relationships form.

These findings come from small studies, and the specifics should be read as indicative of broad patterns, with exact proportions held loosely. The direction is consistent across the sources, even where exact figures carry the usual caution that small samples demand. A container port reaching its commercial market through LinkedIn, and a container line attracting clients through the same network, point to a coherent professional layer that sits apart from the recruitment audience.

## The audiences sit in different places

Read together, these observations describe a market that is segmented by platform. The commercial audience of owners, charterers, ports and suppliers concentrates on professional networks such as LinkedIn, with industry news also moving through Twitter. The recruitment and retention audience of younger seafarers concentrates on more visual and conversational platforms, with Instagram engagement climbing as the workforce turns over. The trade press and the wider market occupy their own mix, overlapping with the commercial layer.

No single platform spans all of these groups. A firm present only on LinkedIn speaks to its commercial counterparts and the shore-side market while remaining largely invisible to the seafarers it hopes to recruit. A firm present only on Instagram reaches a younger workforce while missing the charterers and port partners who make commercial decisions. Coverage of the full audience requires presence across several platforms, each serving the people who gather there.

## The marketing implication

The audiences a maritime firm needs to reach do not sit in the same place. Owners and charterers are reached through professional channels such as LinkedIn. Young officers and ratings, the audience for recruitment and retention, are increasingly reached through Instagram. Ports and the trade press are reached through their own mix of professional networks and industry news channels. Each group has a platform where it concentrates, and those platforms do not coincide.

A channel plan copied from generic B2B advice tends to assume a single professional buyer and a single professional network. That assumption fits part of the maritime market and leaves the rest uncovered. A plan concentrated on one platform, whichever it is, makes the same error from the opposite direction, reaching one segment well and the others not at all. Both approaches miss one or more of the audiences the firm depends on.

Effective maritime channel choice starts from the audience and works back to the platform. The firm identifies which groups it needs to reach, locates where each group gathers, and selects the channels accordingly. The message is then shaped to each platform and each audience, so that the commercial content sits where commercial decisions are made and the recruitment content sits where the future workforce spends its time. The sequence runs from people to platform to message, and the platform mix follows from the audiences it is meant to reach.
