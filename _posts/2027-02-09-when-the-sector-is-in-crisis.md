Disruption tends to clarify what a maritime company's communication is actually for. When schedules hold, ports clear and cargo moves, an audience can absorb a steady diet of brand atmosphere without much consequence. When the system seizes, attention narrows to whoever can explain what is happening and what to do about it. A 2023 study of social media behaviour in shipping during the pandemic offers an unusually direct reading of which content earned attention under exactly those conditions, and the answer carries a clear lesson for how a maritime brand builds its public presence.

Koh, Ng, Wang and Yuen, writing in Technological Forecasting & Social Change (2023), examined 431 COVID-19 Facebook posts published by 18 companies across container liners, dry bulk operators, ship management firms and ports between January 2020 and July 2021. The sample is narrow and specific: one platform, the early pandemic period, a small set of firms. The findings are best read as indicative, with their force limited to that period and that medium. Within those limits, the study applies hierarchical regression to test which content characteristics predicted engagement, measured through the reactions, comments and shares that posts attracted.

## What earned attention during the crisis

Several content types showed a significant positive relationship with engagement. Informational content performed well, as did posts carrying a call to action. Relational content, which builds a sense of connection with the audience, also registered. So did crisis-appeal content, the messaging that addresses self-efficacy, the severity of the situation and the audience's own vulnerability to it. Alongside the substance of the message, the form mattered. Media-richness factors raised engagement, with vividness and what the authors term fluency standing out, and posts that included external links drew stronger response than those that did not.

The pattern that emerges is of an audience under pressure rewarding usefulness. Posts that told people something they needed to know, gave them a concrete next step, acknowledged the gravity of the disruption and pointed onward to more detail performed measurably better. The presence of an external link is worth dwelling on, because it suggests the audience treated these channels as a route to information, a way out toward detail held elsewhere. A post that opens a door to further reading was doing something the audience valued during the crisis.

## What stopped working

Two content types that often carry corporate communication in calmer periods failed to register. Corporate social responsibility content showed a coefficient of 0.046 and was not statistically significant. Purely entertaining content showed a coefficient of 0.051 and was likewise not significant. During the crisis window the study covers, neither feel-good CSR messaging nor entertainment moved engagement in any reliable way.

This is the finding worth sitting with. CSR and entertainment are staples of the corporate content calendar, and in stable conditions they have their place in shaping how a company is perceived. The study suggests that when the operating environment is genuinely disrupted, audiences reallocate their attention toward content that helps them act. A firm that leans on CSR posts and lighter material during a crisis is publishing into a moment when its audience has stopped rewarding that material. The polish remains, but the engagement that justifies it falls away.

Koh and colleagues frame social media in this context as an instrument of reputation and crisis management, a means by which a firm controls its own narrative and curbs the spread of misinformation. That framing fits the engagement data. The content that worked was the content that did the work of a crisis communicator: explaining, directing, acknowledging and linking onward. The content that faded was the content that mainly maintained image.

## What this means for a maritime brand's presence

The practical reading for any maritime firm building its public communication is that a crisis is the period when reputation is most exposed and most decided. The pandemic study captures a moment when audiences sorted content quickly by whether it helped them, and rewarded the clear, informational, link-rich and responsive over the decorative. The container, bulk, ship management and port firms in the sample all faced an audience that had narrowed its attention to whoever could be useful.

That has a direct bearing on how a brand's website and channels should be built, well before any disruption arrives. The infrastructure that carries weight under pressure is structural. A site has to be clear enough to be scanned at speed, organised so that the relevant information is reachable, generous with links to detail and authoritative sources, and responsive enough to update as conditions change. These are properties of the platform itself, designed in advance and ready before the disruption arrives.

A polished CSR presence offers little protection here. A page of community initiatives and sustainability statements communicates values, and the study does not suggest those values are unimportant to a brand over the long run. What it suggests is that such material does not earn engagement at the moment a crisis demands it, and so cannot stand in for a credible, well-structured, responsive presence when one is actually needed. For Clathra and the maritime firms it works with, the implication is that the time to build a communication platform that holds up under strain is the calm period beforehand. The website and channels are the load-bearing parts of a maritime reputation, and disruption is when the load arrives.

The study is a single window, one platform and one crisis, and its coefficients describe that window alone. The direction it points, though, is consistent with how attention behaves when an industry is under strain. Engagement follows usefulness. A maritime brand that has built its presence to be informational, navigable and responsive is positioned to be useful exactly when its audience is deciding whom to trust.
