Shipping is an industry of relationships and intermediaries. Chartering, sale and purchase, and most commercial fixtures are settled through brokers and direct negotiation, with prices arrived at privately and rarely posted. Stopford, in Maritime Economics (3rd ed., Routledge, 2009), documents that the chartering and sale-and-purchase markets transact through broker networks and bilateral bargaining, and Plomaritou and Papadopoulos, in Shipbroking and Chartering Practice (8th ed., Informa/Routledge), set out the same mechanics as the canonical practice text for the trade. The deal itself happens between people who already know each other or who have been introduced through a trusted intermediary. That basic fact shapes everything that follows about where a maritime firm's marketing effort can and cannot reach.

The relationship layer is not incidental to performance. Osobajo, Koliousis and McLaughlin (2021, Journal of Shipping and Trade 6(1)) find that trust, commitment and satisfaction are the variables that drive maritime business performance, and Caliskan and Esmer (2020, Maritime Policy and Management 47(2)) find that relationship marketing builds loyalty and word of mouth among ports and shipping lines. Both studies are specific to the maritime sector, and both point to the same conclusion. The commercial value sits in the relationship and in the reputation that precedes it. The question for any firm spending on a website is what role a digital presence plays when the transaction is conducted offline, between parties for whom personal trust is the deciding factor.

## The decision is largely made before the supplier is contacted

General B2B research describes a buyer who is well advanced before any conversation with a vendor takes place. Gartner reports that 67 percent of B2B buyers prefer a rep-free experience and that buyers spend only around 17 percent of the buying journey meeting with potential suppliers. Forrester (2025) finds that 92 percent of B2B buyers start the process with at least one vendor in mind, and that 41 percent already have a single preferred vendor before formal evaluation begins. These are cross-industry figures, and they apply to shipping by extension, so they are best treated as indicative. The direction is consistent across sources. By the time a buyer makes contact, much of the evaluation has happened privately, and a preferred name is frequently already in place.

That private evaluation is now spread across many surfaces. McKinsey (2022) reports that B2B buyers use around 10 channels through a single purchase, up from 5 in 2016, and that the leading touchpoints include the company website, in-person sales and video. The website sits inside a wider set of channels through which a buyer forms an impression, and it does so during the research phase, before the broker call or the meeting at the stand. The implication is precise. The website is one of the places a counterparty checks while building or confirming a shortlist, and it carries weight in proportion to what it signals about the firm behind it.

## The website is a credibility instrument and a discovery surface

What a buyer takes from a site in those early checks is governed in large part by how the site presents itself. The Stanford Web Credibility Project, reported by Fogg (2002) across around 4,500 participants, found that "design look" was the most-mentioned factor in how people judge a site's credibility, named by about 46 percent of participants. The study is general in scope and does not concern maritime buyers specifically, and the figure should be read as the most-cited factor in credibility judgements, with the understanding that it is one factor among several. The finding is durable. The visual and structural quality of a site is the first thing a visitor reads as a proxy for the seriousness of the organisation.

In a relationship-mediated market, that proxy does real work. Plomaritou and colleagues (2011) report that a charterer's "client information system", meaning how easy a counterparty is to inform, find and verify, is a ranked criterion in the purchase decision. A maritime firm that is hard to find, hard to verify, or that presents a site at odds with the scale of its operations imposes friction at exactly the moment a counterparty is forming a view. A firm that is easy to find and that reads as credible removes that friction. The site does not close the fixture. It supports the verification and the impression that sit underneath the relationship the broker eventually activates.

## What this sets for the budget and the brief

The combined evidence points to a defined job. The transaction happens offline, in broker networks and direct negotiation, among parties for whom trust is the deciding factor. The website operates earlier, as a credibility instrument and a discovery surface used during research, and frequently consulted by a counterparty who already has a name in mind. For a shipping firm, that settles what the site is for and what it should contain. It needs to be findable when a counterparty searches, and it needs to verify the firm quickly and convincingly through clear evidence of what the firm does, who it serves and how it can be reached and checked.

It also settles what the site should cost. A site scoped as a credibility and findability instrument is a different brief from a site scoped as a place where transactions are completed, and it should be budgeted accordingly. Spending built around conversion mechanics that the maritime sales process does not use is spending in the wrong place. Spending built around findability, verification and a presentation that matches the firm's standing supports the part of the process the website actually touches. The marketing question for a shipping firm is therefore narrower and more answerable than it first appears. The site earns its keep by making the firm easy to find and easy to trust during the research that precedes the relationship, and the budget should be set to that purpose.
