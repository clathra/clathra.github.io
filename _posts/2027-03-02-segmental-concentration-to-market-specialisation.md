The question of which market to serve sits ahead of every other commercial decision a shipowner makes. Vessel acquisition, chartering policy, and customer relationships all follow from it. In the marketing literature for shipping, this choice has been described through two contrasting models, and recent survey evidence suggests the relative weight of the two has changed over the past two decades.

## Two models for choosing a market

Plomaritou's work on shipping marketing sets out two ways a tanker owner can frame the market-selection decision.

The first is segmental concentration. Here a firm fixes on a single market segment, defined by cargo type, trade route, or vessel class, and competes to win share within it. The aim is volume inside a defined arena. Rival owners serving the same segment are the direct points of comparison, and competitive position is measured by how much of that segment's business a firm carries. The strategic effort goes into out-competing peers on the terms the segment already rewards, such as freight rate, reliability, and availability of tonnage.

The second is market specialisation. Here a firm selects a narrower niche and specialises to serve the full range of needs within it. The orientation moves from carrying as much segment volume as possible toward covering the requirements of a defined group of charterers across the services they ask for. Depth of service inside the niche becomes the organising principle. A specialised owner builds capability around the particular cargoes, schedules, and standards that its chosen charterers require, and competes on the completeness of that fit.

The two models describe different answers to the same prior question. Segmental concentration treats the segment as the unit and asks how much of it a firm can hold. Market specialisation treats the niche as the unit and asks how completely a firm can serve it.

## What the 2025 survey indicates

The 2026 study by Plomaritou and Hadjiconstantinou in the WMU Journal of Maritime Affairs tracks how surveyed tanker firms described their market selection over the period from 2005 to 2025, and reports a shift in the balance between the two models.

In 2005, segmental concentration was the dominant approach. It was particularly common among small and medium firms, which framed their commercial position in terms of share within a chosen segment. By 2025, market specialisation had become more common across the sample, and the large firms led that movement. The pattern places the larger owners ahead of their smaller peers in adopting a niche-and-serve orientation, with smaller firms more likely to have retained the segment-share framing that prevailed at the start of the period.

Read together, the two readings describe a directional change over twenty years. The starting point in 2005 was a field oriented toward segment share, led there by its smaller participants. The later reading in 2025 shows a field with more weight on niche specialisation, led there by its larger participants.

## Differentiation moved in step

The same study reports two related changes in how surveyed firms differentiated themselves over the period. Differentiation through crew and personnel rose across the sample, and differentiation through corporate image rose as well.

Both shifts are consistent with the move toward specialisation. Serving the full range of a niche's needs places weight on the people who deliver the service and on the reputation that signals fitness to a defined set of charterers. Where a firm competes on how completely it serves a chosen group, the quality of its crew and shore personnel and the standing of its corporate image become part of the offer itself. The parallel rise in these two forms of differentiation sits alongside the market-selection finding and points in the same direction.

## How far the evidence reaches

The survey carries a clear limit, and the authors present it as indicative. It rests on a small, self-reported sample, with thirty firms responding in the 2025 round. Numbers at that scale describe the firms that answered and support no firm generalisation to the wider tanker market. Self-reported descriptions of strategy also reflect how managers characterise their own positioning, which can differ from observed behaviour.

With those limits stated, the survey records a coherent pattern. Across the firms that responded, the centre of gravity in market selection moved from segment share toward niche specialisation between 2005 and 2025, the larger firms moved first, and differentiation through people and through image rose over the same span. The reading is best held as a signal worth watching, with the standing of a settled account of the sector still some way off.

## Specialisation is a marketing decision

A niche strategy only earns its premium if the chosen charterers know the firm as the specialist. A firm can build the capability to serve a defined group completely and still capture none of the value, if the market continues to read it as one more operator competing on rate. The decision to specialise and the work of being recognised as a specialist are the same act, carried out through positioning, reputation, and the way the firm presents itself to the group it has chosen to serve.

The survey records this connection in its own data. Over the same period that market specialisation rose, differentiation through corporate image and through crew and personnel rose with it. Those are marketing expressions of a niche position. A firm that specialises makes the case for its fitness to a defined set of charterers, and it makes that case through the image it projects and the people it puts forward. The strategic shift toward serving a niche shows up, in the firms that have made it, as a shift toward the kind of differentiation that marketing carries.

For an owner weighing the two models, the practical reading is that market selection and market communication are bound together. Segmental concentration competes on terms the segment already rewards, where a firm can compete largely on operational performance and price. Market specialisation competes on fitness to a particular group, which a firm has to establish in the minds of that group before the premium becomes available. The narrower the niche, the more the return depends on being known within it. A specialist that no charterer recognises as a specialist holds the strategy without the standing that makes it pay.
