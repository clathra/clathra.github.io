Ship repair is a difficult thing to buy. The work is technical, the outcome is uncertain until the vessel is back in service, and the cost of a poor choice runs well beyond the invoice through lost trading days and rework. Buyers in this position have little to inspect before they commit, so they fall back on signals they can trust. A study by Virgianti and Ronny (2025), published in the Golden Ratio of Marketing and Applied Psychology of Business, set out to measure which signals actually move the decision.

The authors surveyed 100 owners, agents, and ship managers who had carried out repairs at PT PAL Indonesia, a single state shipyard. They tested three inputs against the decision to purchase repair services: word of mouth, company credibility, and price. The sample is small and drawn from one yard, and the journal sits in a lower tier, so the figures are best read as indicative. The pattern they describe is consistent with how a hard-to-evaluate service tends to get bought.

## What the research found

All three inputs had a direct, significant, and positive effect on the purchase decision. Company credibility carried the largest direct weight at beta 0.23 (p below 0.01), word of mouth followed at beta 0.19 (p equals 0.02), and price came in at beta 0.17 (p equals 0.04). The combined model accounted for about 71 percent of the variance in the decision, a high figure that suggests these three factors capture most of what drives yard selection in the sample.

The more useful finding sits in how each input reaches the decision. The authors also modelled purchase intention as an intermediate step. On intention, price was the strongest driver at beta 0.47, ahead of credibility at 0.27 and word of mouth at 0.17, and intention in turn fed the decision at beta 0.34. Price worked through that channel: it shaped intention, and intention shaped the outcome.

Word of mouth and credibility behaved differently. Their effect on the final decision ran directly, and the test for mediation through intention was not significant. In plain terms, a buyer who hears a trusted account of a yard's work, or who reads a yard as credible, moves toward selecting it without first passing through the deliberative weighing that price triggers. Reputation shortens the path. It acts on the choice itself and bypasses the longer evaluation that price feeds.

## Why reputation does the deciding

The mechanism the authors point to is familiar from the wider services literature. A repair contract is an experience good. Its quality cannot be verified at the point of purchase, only after the steel is cut and the vessel is afloat again. Faced with that uncertainty, buyers look for evidence from others who have already taken the risk. Word of mouth in this setting is not vague chatter. The authors describe it as shared, specific accounts of work quality, reliability against deadlines, pricing, facilities, and service. It substitutes the experience of a peer for an inspection the buyer cannot perform.

Credibility operates on the same logic from a different angle. Where word of mouth carries a peer's verdict, credibility is the yard's own standing, the reputation and image it holds in the eyes of a prospective customer. The authors single this out as a key success factor for a shipyard. A buyer who already regards a yard as established and competent has, in effect, pre-cleared much of the uncertainty before any conversation about scope or price begins.

The two together explain why the price coefficient, while real, sits behind them on the direct path. Price matters once a buyer is weighing options, which is the deliberative work that intention captures. The prior question of which yards make the shortlist at all is settled earlier, on reputation. A yard that is trusted and well regarded enters the contest already ahead, and may never face the price comparison that a less established competitor must win.

## The marketing implication for a yard

The finding has a practical edge for how a repair yard presents itself. If reputation and word of mouth act directly on the decision, the commercial task is to make that reputation legible and checkable. A referral typically starts offline, in a conversation between a ship manager and a peer. The prospect it produces will look for confirmation before committing, and that confirmation now happens online.

This is where the public record carries weight. A yard's documented past repairs, evidence of work delivered on time and to standard, named references willing to stand behind it, and a credible, current website together let a prospect verify the account they were given. The research suggests credibility influences the decision on its own, so the materials that establish credibility are doing direct commercial work in their own right.

The risk runs the other way as well. When word of mouth sends a prospect to a thin or absent web presence, the verification step fails and the credibility chain breaks at the moment it should close. The peer's endorsement created intent; the missing evidence leaves it unconfirmed, and the buyer carries unresolved uncertainty into a high-value decision. A documented record converts the referral that word of mouth started. The absence of one strands it. Practitioner accounts that referred customers convert and retain at higher rates point in the same direction, though those figures are illustrative and sit outside this study.

For a yard, the implication is that reputation is an asset worth recording in public. The trust that a buyer extends to a hard-to-evaluate service is earned in the work and confirmed in the evidence left behind. A record that a prospect can find and check is what allows an offline endorsement to become a contract.
