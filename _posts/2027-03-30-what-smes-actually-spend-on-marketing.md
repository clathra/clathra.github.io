The figure that circulates most often in marketing budget discussions is 7.7 per cent of revenue. It appears in board papers, agency pitches, and trade-press guidance as the share a company ought to spend on marketing. The number is real, and it is widely sourced. The problem is the firms it was measured on. The 7.7 per cent benchmark describes large companies, and small and medium firms that adopt it inherit a planning assumption built for a different scale of business.

## The benchmark and where it comes from

The 7.7 per cent figure is the overall mean reported in The CMO Survey, run by Duke Fuqua together with Deloitte and the American Marketing Association. The same Fall 2024 release breaks marketing spend down by firm size, and the breakdown tells a different story from the headline. Firms under $10M in revenue reported marketing at 16.8 per cent of revenue. Firms in the $10–25M band reported 9.6 per cent, and firms in the $26–99M band reported 6.7 per cent. The share keeps falling as firms grow, reaching 3.4 per cent for firms above $10B. Measured by headcount, the pattern holds: firms under 50 employees reported 17.8 per cent. The smallest revenue band rests on a small sample of around 20 firms, so the precise figure should be read as directional more than exact, although the direction is consistent across both the revenue and the headcount cuts.

The 7.7 per cent mean sits well below the smallest-firm figures because of who answers the survey. The Gartner 2025 CMO Spend Survey, reported via Marketing Brew in 2025, draws on roughly 400 CMOs largely at companies above $1B in revenue. That is an enterprise sample. A mean produced from respondents of that size reflects the spending of large companies, and applying it to a small firm imports a benchmark from the wrong population.

The two readings of the same survey point in opposite directions for a small firm. Read as a single headline, 7.7 per cent looks like a modest, almost cautious target. Read against the size breakdown, it is roughly half of what comparable small firms actually allocate as a share of revenue. The relative intensity of marketing spend is highest at the bottom of the size range and lowest at the top.

## Share is high, absolute spend is small

A higher percentage of a smaller number remains a small number. This is the part the percentage benchmark obscures. The BDC, the Business Development Bank of Canada, reports that small businesses spend on average roughly CAD $30,000 a year on marketing, and it suggests B2B firms allocate 2–5 per cent of revenue. The United States Small Business Administration references average advertising spend around 1.08 per cent and marketing around 7.9 per cent of revenue, and it states plainly that there is no hard and fast rule.

These figures sit alongside each other without resolving into one tidy ratio, and the range itself is the useful finding. The CMO Survey size breakdown shows small firms spending a high share of revenue. The BDC and SBA references show that the absolute and percentage figures land lower once B2B and advertising-specific spending are separated out. Taken together, they describe a small firm working with a marketing budget that is constrained in dollar terms whatever percentage is chosen. A B2B company turning over a few million dollars and allocating in the BDC's 2–5 per cent range is planning around tens of thousands, well below the kind of budget the enterprise benchmark assumes.

That gap between share and absolute amount is where misapplied benchmarks do damage. A small firm that anchors on enterprise spending as a model of what good marketing looks like measures its own modest budget against campaigns, teams, and media buys it was never going to fund. The benchmark sets an aspiration calibrated to companies several orders of magnitude larger, and the small firm reads its own proportionate spending as a shortfall.

## What the proportions mean for a shipping SME

A shipping company of small or medium size faces the same arithmetic. Its marketing budget is small in absolute terms, and the percentage it can defend internally will be modest however the figure is framed. The planning question that follows is about allocation. A constrained budget rewards spending that is proportionate to the firm's size and pointed directly at what brings business in.

For a maritime SME, the activity that meets both tests is a credible, findable web presence. Charterers, brokers, port authorities, and counterparties check a firm online before they engage, and the cost of being credible and discoverable there is low relative to its effect on whether a firm is considered at all. A clear website that states what the firm does, carries proof of safety and reliability, and surfaces in search costs a fraction of an enterprise marketing programme and serves the function a small firm most needs served. That allocation fits a budget measured in tens of thousands. An enterprise-scale build, modelled on the firms the 7.7 per cent figure was actually drawn from, does not.

The evidence base here has limits worth keeping in view. The smallest CMO Survey band is thin, the BDC and SBA references describe broad averages across very different businesses, and none of these figures was produced with maritime firms specifically in mind. They are indicative of a pattern more than precise guidance for any one company. The pattern itself, though, is consistent across the sources: small firms spend a high share on a small base, and the most quoted benchmark was measured on companies they do not resemble. For a shipping SME, that argues for matching marketing investment to the firm's actual scale and concentrating it where a small budget converts, which is being credible and findable to the people who decide whether to do business with it.
